Introduction to Microbiology

Microbiology: Science dealing with living organisms too small to be seen with naked eye.

Medical Microbiology: Study of pathogens, diseases, body defenses, epidemiology, transmission, diagnosis, prevention, treatment, immunity.

Historical Background

Hippocrates: Father of Medicine, environmental factors

Fracastorius (1500): Proposed germ theory

Leeuwenhoek (1632-1723): First observed bacteria

Louis Pasteur (1822-1895): Disproved abiogenesis, pasteurization, vaccines

Robert Koch (1876): Koch's Postulates

Edward Jenner (1798): Smallpox vaccination

Alexander Fleming (1928): Discovered penicillin

Subdivisions of Microbiology

Bacteriology: Study of bacteria
Mycology: Study of fungi
Virology: Study of viruses
Immunology: Study of immune defenses
Parasitology: Study of protozoa/helminths

Classification of Microorganisms

Eukaryotes: Animals, plants, fungi (true nucleus)

Prokaryotes: Bacteria, blue-green algae (primitive nucleus)

Major Microbial Groups

Bacteria: Prokaryotic, binary fission
Fungi: Eukaryotic, yeast or mold forms
Viruses: Smallest (18-300 nm)
Parasites: Most complex, eukaryotic

Bacterial Cell Structure

Morphology & Arrangement

Cocci: Spherical (staphylococci-clusters, streptococci-chains, diplococci-pairs)

Bacilli: Rod-shaped

Spirilla: Spiral-shaped

Other Structures

Capsule: Adherence, protection from phagocytosis

Flagella: Motility (5 types: atrichous to peritrichous)

Pili/Fimbriae: Attachment and genetic transfer

Spores: Resistant survival forms

Plasmids: Extra DNA (virulence, R-plasmids)

Bacterial Nutrition & Growth

Requirements

Water, energy, carbon, nitrogen, inorganic salts, specific environmental conditions.

Environmental Factors

Temperature: Psychrophilic (-5-20°C), Mesophilic (20-45°C), Thermophilic (50-80°C), Hyperthermophilic (>80°C)

Oxygen: Obligate anaerobes, facultative aerobes, obligate aerobes, microaerophilic, aerotolerant

pH: Neutrophilic (pH=7), Acidophilic (pH<7), Alkalophilic (pH>7)

Growth Curve Phases

  1. Lag Phase: Adaptation, no division
  2. Log/Exponential Phase: Rapid division
  3. Stationary Phase: Balance growth/death
  4. Death/Decline Phase: More death than growth

Host-Parasite Relationships

Normal Microbial Flora

Resident Flora: Fixed types in specific areas.

Transient Flora: Temporary nonpathogenic/potentially pathogenic.

Significance: Protective defense, antimicrobial substances, compete with pathogens, synthesize vitamins K/B, can cause infection if misplaced/host compromised.

Pathogenesis Steps

  1. Gain entry (mucous membranes, skin, parenteral)
  2. Adhere via adhesins/ligands
  3. Penetrate and evade defenses
  4. Damage host tissue

Sterilization & Disinfection

Definitions

Sterilization: Complete destruction of all microbes including spores

Disinfection: Destruction of disease-causing microbes (may not kill spores)

Antisepsis: Destruction/inhibition in living tissue

Physical Methods

Autoclave: 121°C, 15 psi, 15 min (moist heat)

Hot Air Oven: 160°C, 2 hours (dry heat)

Pasteurization: 62°C/30min or 72°C/15sec

Filtration: Membrane filters, HEPA filters

Radiation: UV, gamma radiation

Important Bacterial Pathogens

Staphylococcus aureus

  • Gram+ cocci in clusters
  • Catalase+, coagulase+
  • Toxins: enterotoxins, TSST-1, exfoliative
  • Causes: food poisoning, TSS, skin infections, pneumonia
  • Rx: β-lactamase resistant penicillins, vancomycin (MRSA)

Streptococcus pyogenes (Group A)

  • β-hemolytic streptococci
  • Causes: pharyngitis, scarlet fever, cellulitis
  • Sequelae: rheumatic fever, glomerulonephritis
  • Lab: β-hemolysis, bacitracin sensitive

Streptococcus pneumoniae

  • α-hemolytic, lancet-shaped diplococci
  • Encapsulated, optochin sensitive
  • Causes: pneumonia, otitis media, meningitis

Enterococcus

  • E. faecalis, E. faecium most common
  • Normal enteric flora
  • Causes: UTI, wounds, bacteremia, endocarditis
  • Common nosocomial infections

Coagulase-Negative Staphylococci

  • S. epidermidis: prosthetic infections
  • S. saprophyticus: UTI in young women
  • S. haemolyticus: bloodstream infections

Key Concepts & Principles

Koch's Postulates

  1. Microbe in every case of disease
  2. Isolated in pure culture from lesions
  3. Reproduces disease in lab animal
  4. Re-isolated from experimental animal

Germ Theory of Disease

Specific diseases caused by specific microorganisms (Pasteur, Koch).

Spontaneous Generation vs Biogenesis

Abiogenesis: Life from non-living (disproved by Pasteur)

Biogenesis: Life from pre-existing life (proven)
