Staphylococcus

Visual Memory Aid

Imagine a bunch of purple grapes (clusters) with golden jewelry (aureus = golden). The grapes are sticky (adherence) and have protective wax coating (coagulase).

Staphylococcus aureus

Gram-Positive Cocci in Clusters Facultative Anaerobe

Key Identification

Coagulase-positive, catalase-positive, golden-yellow colonies, beta-hemolytic on blood agar

Virulence Factors

Protein A (binds IgG Fc), coagulase, hyaluronidase, toxins (TSST-1, enterotoxins, exfoliative toxins)

Diseases

Skin infections (boils, cellulitis), pneumonia, endocarditis, osteomyelitis, TSS, food poisoning

Treatment

Beta-lactams (if sensitive), MRSA: vancomycin, linezolid, daptomycin

Memory Mnemonic

"AUREUS Attacks Usually Require Effective Understanding of Sources"

  • A - Abscesses, Adherence factors
  • U - UTI (rare), Universal colonizer (nares)
  • R - Resistance (MRSA, penicillinase)
  • E - Enterotoxins (food poisoning)
  • U - Unusual toxins (TSST-1, exfoliative)
  • S - Skin infections (most common)

Coagulase-Negative Staphylococci (CoNS)

Gram-Positive Cocci in Clusters Facultative Anaerobe

Key Species

S. epidermidis (most common), S. saprophyticus (UTI in young women), S. haemolyticus

Virulence Factors

Biofilm formation (slime layer), adherence to prosthetic materials, novobiocin resistance (S. saprophyticus)

Diseases

Device-related infections, UTI (S. saprophyticus), endocarditis (prosthetic valves), bacteremia

Treatment

Vancomycin for serious infections, removal of infected device often required

Memory Mnemonic

"CoNS Can't Open New Safes"

  • C - Coagulase-Negative
  • O - Opportunistic pathogens
  • N - Novobiocin test (S. sapro resistant)
  • S - Skin normal flora

Streptococcus

Visual Memory Aid

Imagine a chain of red beads (strepto = chain) with different colored clasps: Group A has an A+ grade, Group B has a baby rattle, Pneumococcus has a helmet (capsule).

Streptococcus pyogenes (Group A)

Gram-Positive Cocci in Chains Facultative Anaerobe

Key Identification

Beta-hemolytic, bacitracin-sensitive, ASO antibodies in serum, catalase-negative

Virulence Factors

M protein (anti-phagocytic), hyaluronic acid capsule, streptolysins, pyrogenic exotoxins, streptokinase

Diseases

Pharyngitis, impetigo, cellulitis, necrotizing fasciitis, scarlet fever, rheumatic fever, glomerulonephritis

Treatment

Penicillin (drug of choice), macrolides if allergic, 10-day course to prevent rheumatic fever

Memory Mnemonic

"PYOGENES Produces Your Ordinary Germs Except Notable Serious sequelae"

  • P - Pharyngitis (strep throat)
  • Y - Yellow pus (pyogenic)
  • O - Otitis media (less common)
  • G - Glomerulonephritis (post-strep)
  • E - Erysipelas (skin infection)
  • N - Necrotizing fasciitis
  • E - Erythrogenic toxin (scarlet fever)
  • S - Sequelae (rheumatic fever, glomerulonephritis)

Streptococcus agalactiae (Group B)

Gram-Positive Cocci in Chains Facultative Anaerobe

Key Identification

Beta-hemolytic, bacitracin-resistant, CAMP test positive, hippurate hydrolysis positive

Virulence Factors

Polysaccharide capsule (sialic acid), hemolysin, CAMP factor, adherence proteins

Diseases

Neonatal meningitis/sepsis, UTI in pregnant women, chorioamnionitis, bacteremia in elderly

Treatment

Penicillin or ampicillin, intrapartum prophylaxis for colonized mothers

Memory Palace Technique

Imagine a baby's nursery with "GBS" on the wall. The baby has:

  • A blue blanket (beta-hemolytic but weaker than Group A)
  • A resistant pacifier (bacitracin-resistant)
  • A CAMPing bag (CAMP test positive)
  • A mother's vaginal swab on the table (screening at 35-37 weeks)

Streptococcus pneumoniae (Pneumococcus)

Gram-Positive Lancet-shaped Diplococci Facultative Anaerobe

Key Identification

Alpha-hemolytic, optochin-sensitive, bile-soluble, Quellung reaction positive

Virulence Factors

Polysaccharide capsule (90+ serotypes), pneumolysin, IgA protease, autolysin

Diseases

Pneumonia (lobar), otitis media, meningitis, sinusitis, bacteremia

Treatment

Penicillin (if sensitive), 3rd gen cephalosporins, vancomycin for meningitis, PCV13/PPSV23 vaccines

Lobar Pneumonia

Think of a lung filled with rusty sputum (classic sign)

Otitis Media

Imagine a child pulling ear with pneumococcus inside

Meningitis

Picture lancet-shaped bacteria in CSF with neutrophils

Enterococcus

Visual Memory Aid

Imagine tough survivors in a harsh environment (bowel) wearing bulletproof vests (antibiotic resistance). They're moving from the intestine (entero) to the urinary tract via catheters.

Enterococcus faecalis & E. faecium

Gram-Positive Cocci in Pairs/Chains Facultative Anaerobe

Key Identification

Gamma-hemolytic, PYR positive, bile-esculin positive, growth in 6.5% NaCl, Group D antigen

Virulence Factors

Intrinsic antibiotic resistance, biofilm formation, cytolysin, aggregation substance

Diseases

UTI (catheter-associated), biliary tract infections, endocarditis (subacute), wound infections

Treatment

Ampicillin + aminoglycoside (synergy), vancomycin, VRE: linezolid, daptomycin, tigecycline

Memory Mnemonic

"ENTERO: Extremely Nasty Tough Resistant Organisms"

  • E - Endocarditis (subacute)
  • N - Nosocomial (hospital-acquired)
  • T - Tough (survives harsh conditions)
  • E - Enteric origin (normal gut flora)
  • R - Resistant (VRE, intrinsic resistance)
  • O - Opportunistic pathogens
Step 1: Normal Flora

Enterococci are normal inhabitants of the GI tract

Step 2: Translocation

Move to urinary tract via catheters or to blood via procedures

Step 3: Infection

Cause UTI, bacteremia, or endocarditis (often on damaged valves)

Step 4: Treatment Challenge

Intrinsic resistance makes treatment difficult (VRE concerns)

Laboratory Tests

Key Diagnostic Tests

Catalase Test

Differentiates Staphylococcus (+) from Streptococcus (-)
Add H₂O₂ to bacterial colony → bubbles = positive (Staph)

Memory: "CATalase for CATS (Staph)" or "Staph has CATs"

Coagulase Test

Differentiates S. aureus (+) from other Staph (-)
Mix bacteria with plasma → clot formation = positive

Memory: "AUREUS CLOTS" or "Golden clots"

Bacitracin Test

Identifies Group A Strep (S. pyogenes) - sensitive
0.04 unit disk → zone of inhibition = Group A

Memory: "A for Antibiotic (bacitracin)"

Optochin Test

Identifies S. pneumoniae - sensitive
Ethylhydrocupreine disk → zone ≥14mm = pneumococcus

Memory: "Pneumo is OPTimistic (optochin sensitive)"

Bile Solubility Test

Confirms S. pneumoniae - soluble in bile
Add sodium deoxycholate → colony lysis = pneumococcus

Memory: "Pneumo dissolves in bile like soap"

CAMP Test

Identifies Group B Strep (S. agalactiae) - positive
Streak perpendicular to S. aureus → arrowhead hemolysis = Group B

Memory: "B for Baby, CAMP for babies"

Clinical Manifestations

Respiratory Infections

Pharyngitis

S. pyogenes (Group A Strep)
Exudative tonsils, fever, cervical LAD, NO cough

Treat: Penicillin x10 days

Pneumonia

S. pneumoniae (most common)
Rusty sputum, high fever, pleuritic pain, lobar consolidation

Treat: PCN or cephalosporins

Pneumonia (other)

S. aureus (post-influenza)
Rapid progression, cavitary lesions, empyema

Treat: Anti-staphylococcal PCN

Skin & Soft Tissue Infections

Impetigo

S. aureus OR S. pyogenes
Honey-colored crusts, non-bullous (Strep) or bullous (Staph)

Cellulitis/Erysipelas

S. pyogenes (raised borders)
Bright red, warm, tender, well-demarcated (erysipelas)

Abscess/Furuncle

S. aureus (most common)
Purulent collection, fluctuant, needs drainage

Necrotizing Fasciitis

S. pyogenes (Type II)
Severe pain out of proportion, bullae, crepitus, sepsis

Systemic Infections

Endocarditis

Acute: S. aureus (destructive, normal valves)
Subacute: Viridans Strep, Enterococcus (damaged valves)

Meningitis

Neonates: S. agalactiae (GBS)
Adults: S. pneumoniae (most common)
Post-neurosurgery: S. aureus

Osteomyelitis

Children: S. aureus (hematogenous, metaphysis)
Vertebral: S. aureus (adults, IV drug use)

Toxic Shock Syndrome

S. aureus (TSST-1)
Fever, rash, hypotension, multi-organ involvement

Treatment & Resistance

Antibiotic Choices

S. aureus (MSSA)

1st line: Anti-staphylococcal penicillin (nafcillin, oxacillin)
Alternatives: 1st gen cephalosporin (cefazolin), clindamycin, vancomycin

Resistance: Penicillinase in 90% (use penicillinase-resistant drugs)

S. aureus (MRSA)

1st line: Vancomycin
Alternatives: Linezolid, daptomycin, TMP-SMX, doxycycline, ceftaroline

Resistance: mecA gene → altered PBP2a

S. pyogenes

1st line: Penicillin V or amoxicillin x10 days
If PCN allergic: Macrolide (azithromycin), clindamycin, cephalexin (if non-anaphylactic)

Always sensitive to penicillin!

S. pneumoniae

Non-meningeal: Penicillin (if sensitive), amoxicillin, ceftriaxone
Meningitis: Ceftriaxone + vancomycin + dexamethasone

Resistance: Altered PBPs → PRSP

Key Resistance Mechanisms

Beta-lactamase

Organisms: S. aureus (penicillinase), some Enterococcus
Mechanism: Enzyme hydrolyzes beta-lactam ring
Overcome by: Beta-lactamase inhibitors (clavulanate, sulbactam)

Altered PBPs

Organisms: MRSA (PBP2a), PRSP
Mechanism: PBPs have low affinity for beta-lactams
Overcome by: Vancomycin, linezolid, daptomycin

Vancomycin Resistance

Organisms: VRE (E. faecium > E. faecalis), VRSA (rare)
Mechanism: Change D-ala-D-ala to D-ala-D-lactate
Overcome by: Linezolid, daptomycin, tigecycline

Efflux Pumps

Organisms: Some S. aureus, S. pneumoniae
Mechanism: Actively pump antibiotic out of cell
Overcome by: Higher doses, combination therapy

Resistance Memory Hook

"MRSA Makes Really Serious Admissions"

  • M - mecA gene
  • R - Resistance to all beta-lactams
  • S - S. aureus
  • A - Altered PBP2a

"VRE: Very Resistant Enterococci" - remember they're from the gut (entero) and very tough

Exam Strategy

  • First, identify Gram stain and morphology (clusters vs chains)
  • Second, think about catalase (Staph vs Strep)
  • Third, consider hemolysis pattern (alpha, beta, gamma)
  • Fourth, recall key tests (coagulase, bacitracin, optochin, etc.)
  • Fifth, associate with clinical presentation
  • Finally, determine treatment considering resistance patterns

Must-Know Facts

  • Catalase: Staph +, Strep -
  • Coagulase: Only S. aureus +
  • Bacitracin S: Group A Strep
  • Optochin S: S. pneumoniae
  • PYR +: Enterococcus & Group A Strep
  • Always treat Strep pyogenes x10 days
  • MRSA = Vancomycin

Common Pitfalls

  • Don't confuse S. aureus with CoNS
  • Remember: S. pneumo is alpha-hemolytic (green)
  • GBS is bacitracin-resistant (not sensitive)
  • Enterococcus is PYR + (like Group A Strep)
  • Novobiocin R = S. saprophyticus (UTI in women)
  • VRE is most commonly E. faecium
